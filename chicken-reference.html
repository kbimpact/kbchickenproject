Easter Egger — full notes
  • Pea comb gives cold protection comparable to rose comb — no frostbite spike to worry about. Solid Zone 5b bird.
  • Good layers (~250/year), cold-hardy, docile, easy to manage. NPIP-certified at The Chick Hatchery.
  • Tradeoff: colored eggs (blue/green/pink), not brown. If you don't care about egg color uniformity, this is the clearest fallback.
  • Not a recognized APA breed — "Easter Egger" is a mixed-heritage bird carrying the blue egg gene. Individual birds vary in appearance.
Rhode Island Red — full notes
  • Excellent layer — 250–300 brown eggs/year, one of the highest-production heritage breeds available.
  • Cold-hardy body and heavy feathering. But single comb is the problem: tall, upright, with multiple points. Vulnerable to frostbite in Wisconsin winters even with good ventilation.
  • Workable but requires more diligent humidity management in the coop. Comb damage in severe cold is a real possibility.
  • If you go RIR, monitor comb weekly in winter and apply Vaseline on days below 10°F as a preventive measure.
  • Bottom line: good bird, wrong comb for Zone 5b. Use only if Wyandotte and Easter Egger both fail.

Ventilation

Critical Most under-designed element of coops. Frostbite is caused by humidity above 75% — not cold alone. Do not seal the coop in winter.
  • Holes near ceiling, north and south walls
  • Cold: close north holes, keep south open unless bitter cold
  • Warm: open all holes and windows — cross-ventilate
  • Chickens produce CO2, moisture, ammonia — stale air concentrates disease

Predator Management

Known threats: hawk · fox · owl · raccoon · weasel · mink · skunk · rat · cat · dog · coyote · opossum · snake · bobcat

Key Controls

  • Automatic pop hole closer — closes at dusk, opens at dawn
  • Hardware cloth (not chicken wire) on ventilation — 1.5 in mesh
  • Concrete apron or hardware cloth skirt — stops digging predators
  • Electric fence — 6 in and 12 in wires, bent outward

Seasonal Management

Winter — Cold and Frostbite

Key Fact Frostbite is caused by humidity above 75% — not cold alone. Ventilation is the fix. Do not seal the coop in winter.
  • 2×2 perches allow birds to cover toes with body feathers at night
  • Heat lamp only if ceiling under 7 ft and temps very low — thermostat at 35°F, wire guard on lamp

Winter Lighting

✅ Decision Made No supplemental lighting. Accepting reduced winter production. Natural rest cycle — may improve longevity.

Summer — Heat Stress

Warning Chickens cannot regulate body temp above 104°F. Heat stress can be lethal.
  • Cool water at all times · extra stations · electrolytes in water
  • Do not medicate water during heat stress
  • A fan moving air to 90°F = still temp of 70°F
  • Feed scratch in the morning only — not afternoon

Manure Management

  • ~1 lb per bird per week · 9 birds = ~36–40 lbs/month
  • Higher nitrogen than horse, cow, or goat manure
  • Damp litter releases ammonia — aerate frequently, remove damp spots immediately
  • Ammonia control: 1 lb ground limestone/week stirred into litter
  • Fly control: frequent cleanout · bleach scrubdown (1 tbsp/gallon) weekly in fly season
  • Biological fly predators (parasitic wasps) — release mid to late May before flies establish
  • Composting: active at 12–15 in deep with carbon material · garden: 45 lbs per 100 sq ft

Health & Biosecurity

Signs of a Healthy Bird

Active · alert · full wavy comb · bright shiny eyes · talking and clucking · normal droppings (firm, grayish-brown with white urate cap). Cecal droppings (mustard-colored, foamy) are normal once or twice daily.

Warning Signs

Weight loss or production decline · labored breathing · coughing/sneezing/nasal discharge · staggering or paralysis · sudden death · any change in droppings

Biosecurity

  • Isolate new birds at least 2 weeks before introducing
  • Change shoes before visiting another flock — require the same of visitors
  • Keep wild birds away from coop and feed
  • Do not mix birds of different ages
  • Medicate only when necessary — routine use creates drug-resistant organisms

Parasites

Internal — worms and coccidia
  • Cecal worms — most common
  • Gapeworms — more likely with guineas present. Wrap around windpipe. Can kill.
  • Large roundworms — weakness, weight loss, pale heads
  • Do NOT deworm indiscriminately — submit fecal samples every 3 months. A test costs a few dollars.
  • Coccidia — most common cause of death in young birds. Good sanitation + ask vet about anticoccidial timing.
External — mites and lice
  • Northern fowl mites — active year-round, worst in winter. Primary Wisconsin concern.
  • Lice — white eggs at feather bases
  • Treatment: diatomaceous earth in dust bath, linseed oil on roosts and cracks

First-Aid Kit — Assemble Before Birds Arrive

Saline wound wash · hydrogen peroxide · gauze pads and rolls · tweezers · syringe for flushing · Wonder Dust (bleeding control) · Betadine/povidone-iodine · Desitin or Ichthammol · hydrocortisone cream · KY Jelly · Vaseline · electrolyte powder · vet wrap/first-aid tape · tongue depressors (splints) · zip bags (droppings collection) · paper towels · clean container

Guineas — Key Differences

  • Roost higher than chickens — will claim top perches · plan accordingly
  • Better free-rangers than confined birds — significant outdoor time required daily
  • Do not use nest boxes — eggs laid on the ground, often hidden
  • Harder to confine — wing clipping relevant · fence planning must account for this
  • Require training to return to coop at dusk when new to a property
  • Two waterers and two feeders required — compete at single stations
  • Gapeworms more likely with guineas present — flag for monitoring

Do Not Wash Washing removes the bloom — a natural antimicrobial coating that keeps bacteria out through the shell's pores. Don't wash until just before use.

Preservation Methods

Oiling

Coat clean, room-temperature eggs in light mineral oil. Drain on a rack for at least 30 minutes.

Thermostabilization

Submerge in water at exactly 130°F — 15 minutes for room-temp eggs, 18 minutes for cold. Cool immediately in tap water. Destroys surface bacteria without cooking the egg.

Freezing

Remove from shell before freezing. Yolks: add 1/8 tsp salt or 1.5 tsp corn syrup per 4 yolks to prevent gumminess. Thaw in refrigerator overnight. Use only in fully cooked dishes.
